Middle Bronze Age

Bronze Age · Prehistory (~2,100–1,550 BCE)

If the Early Bronze Age built the first cities, the Middle Bronze Age connects them. This is when the ancient world stops being a collection of isolated civilizations and becomes, for the first time, a genuinely interconnected system.

The world goes global

Trade routes in the Middle Bronze Age link Egypt, Mesopotamia, the Indus Valley, the Aegean, and Anatolia into a single economic web. Archaeologists find Egyptian scarabs in Mesopotamian graves, Indus Valley standardized weights in Persian Gulf trading ports, Cretan pottery in Egyptian tombs. These aren't curiosities — they're evidence of regular, institutionalized exchange across thousands of miles.

The mechanism was merchant networks operating out of trading colonies. The Old Assyrian merchants of Kanesh (in modern Turkey) left behind thousands of clay tablets documenting their operations — credit arrangements, partnership disputes, market prices, family letters. It reads less like ancient history and more like a business archive. Commerce, at scale, is already fully recognizable.

Three key developments

1. The first law codes

Hammurabi's Code (~1,754 BCE) — 282 laws carved into a basalt stele — covers wages, divorce, professional liability, property rights, and criminal punishment. It's not the first law code (Ur-Nammu's predates it by ~350 years), but it's the most complete. What it reveals: a society complex enough to need written rules adjudicating disputes between strangers. Law is what you invent when you can no longer rely on everyone knowing everyone else.

2. Imperial consolidation

The Middle Bronze Age sees the first real empires — not just dominant city-states but entities actively projecting power over distant territories. Babylon rises under Hammurabi to control most of Mesopotamia. Egypt's Middle Kingdom expands deep into Nubia, establishing fortified garrisons and extracting gold that will fund centuries of Egyptian power. The logic of empire — conquer, extract, administer — is now fully operational.

3. Disruption and adaptation

The period isn't smooth. Around 2,200 BCE, a prolonged drought (the 4.2 kiloyear event) collapses the Old Kingdom of Egypt and severely weakens Mesopotamian states. The Hyksos — a people from the Levant — infiltrate and eventually rule northern Egypt, introducing the horse-drawn chariot that will transform warfare for a thousand years. Disruption keeps forcing adaptation, and adaptation keeps producing new capabilities.

The Indus Valley at its peak

Often overlooked in Western-centric accounts, the Indus Valley civilization reaches its height in this period. Cities like Mohenjo-daro and Harappa are among the largest on Earth — highly planned, with sophisticated drainage systems, standardized brick sizes, and what appears to be a relatively egalitarian social structure (few signs of palaces or warrior burials). Their writing system remains undeciphered. They collapse around 1,900 BCE, possibly from climate shifts disrupting the monsoon cycle, and remain something of a historical enigma.

Why it matters

The Middle Bronze Age demonstrates that civilization's great vulnerability is also its great strength: interdependence. Connected societies grow faster, innovate more, and recover from local shocks better than isolated ones. But they also transmit shocks farther and faster. That tension — between the benefits and fragility of integration — will recur at every subsequent scale of human organization, right up to the present.
