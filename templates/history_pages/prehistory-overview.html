Paleolithic

Stone Age · Prehistory

The Paleolithic is the longest chapter in human history by far — roughly 3.3 million years, compared to the mere ~10,000 years since. Here's the core of it.

What it was

"Paleolithic" means old stone in Greek. It covers the entire span from when our ancestors first chipped rocks into tools until the end of the last Ice Age (~12,000 BCE). For most of this time, humans weren't anatomically modern — we share this period with Homo habilis, Homo erectus, Neanderthals, and others.

Three key themes

1. Stone tools as the defining technology

The whole period is essentially measured by tool sophistication — from crude choppers (Oldowan industry, ~2.5M BCE) to finely flaked hand-axes (Acheulean, ~1.7M BCE) to delicate blade tools (Upper Paleolithic, ~50,000 BCE). It's slow progress by any modern standard, but it's the foundation of everything.

2. Hunter-gatherer life

No farming, no permanent settlements. Small bands (~20–50 people) followed game and seasonal plants. This lifestyle was enormously successful — it's how humans colonized every continent except Antarctica.

3. The cognitive revolution (~70,000–40,000 BCE)

Something shifted in the Upper Paleolithic — cave paintings at Lascaux and Altamira, carved figurines, musical instruments, long-distance trade in shells and ochre. Abstract thought, symbolic behavior, and what we'd recognize as culture suddenly appear in the archaeological record. Nobody fully agrees on why it happened.

Why it matters

Our brains, bodies, and many of our social instincts were shaped almost entirely during this period. The agricultural revolution came so recently that evolution had almost no time to adapt us to it — in a very real sense, we're still Paleolithic animals running on Stone Age hardware.
