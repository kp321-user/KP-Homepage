The American Revolution (1765–1783)

The American Revolution was a political and military struggle in which the Thirteen Colonies in North America won independence from Great Britain and established the United States of America.

Background

By the mid-18th century, Great Britain controlled thirteen colonies along the eastern coast of North America.

After the French and Indian War (1754–1763), Britain faced heavy debts and sought to raise revenue from the colonies through new taxes.

Many colonists objected because they had no representatives in the British Parliament.

Major Sources of Tension

  • The Stamp Act (1765) taxed printed materials.
  • The Townshend Acts (1767) imposed duties on imported goods.
  • The Tea Act (1773) granted special privileges to the British East India Company.
  • Colonists protested "taxation without representation."

Growing tensions led to boycotts, protests, and occasional violence.

The Road to Revolution

The Boston Massacre (1770)

British soldiers fired into a crowd in Boston, killing five colonists. The event became a powerful symbol of British oppression.

The Boston Tea Party (1773)

Colonists disguised as Indigenous people boarded British ships and dumped tea into Boston Harbor in protest against British taxation.

The Intolerable Acts (1774)

In response, Britain imposed harsh measures on Massachusetts, including closing Boston Harbor.

The colonies united in opposition and convened the First Continental Congress.

The War Begins (1775)

Fighting broke out in April 1775 at the Battles of Lexington and Concord in Massachusetts.

Colonial militias confronted British troops, marking the beginning of the Revolutionary War.

The Second Continental Congress soon met and organized a continental army under George Washington.

Declaration of Independence (1776)

On July 4, 1776, the Continental Congress adopted the Declaration of Independence, primarily written by Thomas Jefferson.

The document declared:

  • The colonies were independent states.
  • All people possess natural rights.
  • Governments derive power from the consent of the governed.
  • People have the right to replace governments that violate their rights.

The Revolutionary War (1775–1783)

Early Challenges

The Continental Army faced significant difficulties:

  • Limited supplies and equipment.
  • Inexperienced soldiers.
  • A powerful British military.

Despite setbacks, Washington managed to keep the army intact.

Turning Point: Saratoga (1777)

The American victory at the Battle of Saratoga convinced France that the colonies had a realistic chance of winning.

France entered the war as an ally, providing:

  • Troops
  • Naval support
  • Weapons
  • Financial assistance

This alliance greatly strengthened the American cause.

Winter at Valley Forge (1777–1778)

The Continental Army endured a difficult winter at Valley Forge.

Although conditions were harsh, training under Baron von Steuben improved the army's discipline and effectiveness.

Yorktown (1781)

The decisive battle occurred at Yorktown, Virginia.

American and French forces trapped the British army under General Charles Cornwallis.

Cornwallis surrendered in October 1781, effectively ending major military operations.

Treaty of Paris (1783)

The war formally ended with the Treaty of Paris in 1783.

Britain recognized the independence of the United States and granted territory extending west to the Mississippi River.

Results and Legacy

The American Revolution had lasting effects on both America and the world.

Major Outcomes

  • The United States became an independent nation.
  • Republican government replaced colonial rule.
  • The U.S. Constitution and Bill of Rights were eventually established.
  • Revolutionary ideas about liberty and self-government spread internationally.
  • The Revolution influenced later movements, including the French Revolution.

Key Leaders

  • George Washington – Commander of the Continental Army and later the first U.S. President.
  • Thomas Jefferson – Principal author of the Declaration of Independence.
  • Benjamin Franklin – Diplomat who secured French support.
  • John Adams – Revolutionary leader and later the second U.S. President.

In One Sentence

The American Revolution was the successful struggle of the Thirteen Colonies to gain independence from Great Britain and establish the United States as a self-governing republic based on the principles of liberty and representative government.
