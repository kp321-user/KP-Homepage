# Mesolithic
### Stone Age · Prehistory

The Mesolithic is the awkward middle child of the Stone Age — short, transitional, and often skipped over. But it's genuinely fascinating because it's when humans had to *reinvent* how to live.

---

### What triggered it

Around 12,000 BCE, the last Ice Age ended. Glaciers retreated, sea levels rose (flooding coastal settlements we'll never excavate), forests spread across open tundra, and the megafauna that Paleolithic hunters depended on — woolly mammoths, giant elk, cave lions — went extinct. The world humans had adapted to for millions of years simply disappeared. The Mesolithic is the response.

---

### Three key themes

**1. Microliths — the signature technology**

Instead of large hand-axes, Mesolithic toolmakers produced tiny, precise flint blades (*microliths*) that were hafted into composite tools — arrows, harpoons, sickles. It's a shift from brute force to engineering. Same raw material, radically different logic.

**2. Intensified relationship with the environment**

With big game gone, diets diversified enormously — shellfish, fish, birds, small mammals, nuts, berries. Middens (ancient rubbish heaps) from this period are treasure troves for archaeologists: they reveal extraordinarily varied, often nutritious diets. In some regions, people were semi-sedentary, returning seasonally to the same rich sites.

**3. The bridge to farming**

The Mesolithic isn't just a waiting room for the Neolithic. In some places — notably the Levant — you can watch the transition happen in the archaeological record. People start managing wild grain stands, taming dogs (the first domesticated animal appears here), and clustering around reliable food sources. The idea of *controlling* nature, rather than just following it, starts here.

---

### A specific window: Doggerland

One of the most evocative Mesolithic stories involves a place that no longer exists. Doggerland was a vast landmass connecting Britain to continental Europe — fertile plains, rivers, wetlands, home to thriving Mesolithic communities. Between ~8,000–6,000 BCE, rising sea levels swallowed it entirely. It's now the North Sea floor. Fishermen still occasionally dredge up Mesolithic tools and animal bones from it.

---

### Why it matters

The Mesolithic proves that humans aren't just passive responders to their environment — they're adaptive problem-solvers. Faced with total ecological collapse, they didn't disappear. They retooled, literally and figuratively. That resilience is the thread that runs directly into the agricultural revolution that follows.
