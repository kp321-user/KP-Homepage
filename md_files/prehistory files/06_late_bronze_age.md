# Late Bronze Age
### Bronze Age · Prehistory (~1,550–1,200 BCE)

The Late Bronze Age is the ancient world at its most sophisticated — and its most fragile. It represents the first genuine international order in human history, and its collapse is one of the most dramatic systems failures the world has ever seen.

---

### The international system

By 1,400 BCE, the Eastern Mediterranean operates as a community of peer powers. Egypt under Ramesses II, the Hittite Empire in Anatolia, Mycenaean Greece, Assyria, Babylon, and the Aegean trading states exchange ambassadors, sign treaties, intermarry royal families, and trade constantly. The Egyptian-Hittite peace treaty of 1,259 BCE — signed after the Battle of Kadesh, history's first recorded major land battle — is the oldest surviving international treaty. Both sides claimed victory. Both sides wanted peace. Sound familiar?

The correspondence between these powers survives in the Amarna Letters — clay tablets found in Egypt recording diplomatic exchanges in Akkadian (the diplomatic *lingua franca* of the day). Kings address each other as "brother," request gold, complain about delays in shipments, and negotiate marriages with a frankness that feels remarkably modern.

---

### Three key developments

**1. The cosmopolitan economy**

The Uluburun shipwreck, discovered off Turkey's coast and dating to ~1,300 BCE, gives us a perfect snapshot. A single ship carrying: copper and tin ingots, ebony logs, glass ingots, ivory, spices, Canaanite wine jars, Egyptian scarabs, Mycenaean pottery, and artifacts from at least seven different cultures. This wasn't unusual — it was routine commerce. The Late Bronze Age world was staggeringly cosmopolitan by any pre-modern standard.

**2. Military technology matures**

The chariot — introduced via the Hyksos in the Middle Bronze Age — reaches full tactical sophistication here. Kadesh (~1,274 BCE) involved thousands of chariots on each side. Alongside chariots: bronze armor, composite bows, sophisticated siege techniques. Warfare becomes professionalized and expensive, which means only large, surplus-generating states can afford to wage it seriously. Military power and economic power become inseparable.

**3. Mycenaean Greece**

The Greek-speaking Mycenaeans are the Late Bronze Age's most consequential late entrants. Their palace economies — centered at Mycenae, Tiryns, Pylos — produce Linear B, the earliest written Greek (used, like cuneiform before it, primarily for accounting). Their traders and warriors range across the Mediterranean. The legends of the Trojan War, whatever their historical kernel, belong to this world. When Mycenaean civilization collapses, Greece enters a 400-year dark age and loses literacy entirely — one of history's starkest examples of how fragile accumulated knowledge can be.

---

### The collapse (~1,200–1,150 BCE)

Within roughly 50 years, nearly every major palace civilization around the Eastern Mediterranean either collapsed or severely contracted. The checklist is stunning: Mycenaean Greece — gone. The Hittite Empire — gone. Ugarit, the great Levantine trading city — burned and never rebuilt. Its last known tablet, found still in the kiln where it was being fired when the city fell, is a desperate request for military aid that never arrived. Egypt survived but shrank dramatically and never recovered its former reach.

The cause remains genuinely debated among historians. The leading hypothesis is a *systems collapse* — a perfect storm of prolonged drought, disrupted trade networks, internal rebellions, and the "Sea Peoples" (migrating groups who appear in Egyptian records attacking from the sea) hitting an over-integrated, brittle international system simultaneously. No single cause broke it. The interconnectedness that made the system powerful had also made it fragile. When enough nodes failed, the whole network went down.

---

### Why it matters

The Late Bronze Age Collapse is a case study in how complexity creates vulnerability. The more integrated and efficient a system becomes, the more damage a simultaneous multi-point failure can do. It's a pattern that recurs — the fall of Rome, the 2008 financial crisis, pandemic supply chain failures. The Bronze Age version just happened to set back Mediterranean civilization by several centuries. The Iron Age that follows isn't a continuation — it's a rebuilding from rubble, with new peoples, new technologies, and crucially, a new and more democratic material at its center.
