# Early Bronze Age
### Bronze Age · Prehistory (~3,300–2,100 BCE)

The Early Bronze Age is where the Neolithic experiment pays off — spectacularly. Farming surpluses, accumulated over millennia, finally cross a threshold and crystallize into something new: the first cities, the first writing, the first states.

---

### The metallurgical leap

Bronze is an alloy — roughly 90% copper, 10% tin. Neither metal alone is particularly useful; together they produce something harder than either, that holds an edge, and can be cast into precise shapes. The catch is that copper and tin rarely occur in the same place, which means bronze production *requires trade networks* spanning hundreds or thousands of miles. The Bronze Age doesn't just introduce a new material — it introduces economic interdependence between distant societies for the first time.

---

### Three key developments

**1. The first cities**

Mesopotamia's Sumerian city-states — Uruk, Ur, Lagash — are the headline act. Uruk by 3,000 BCE may have held 50,000 people, making it the largest settlement in human history to that point. What made it possible: irrigation agriculture on the Tigris-Euphrates floodplain producing massive grain surpluses, managed by temple bureaucracies that ran the economy as much as they ran religion.

**2. The invention of writing**

Cuneiform — wedge-shaped marks pressed into clay tablets — was invented not for literature but for accounting. The first written texts are grain receipts and tax records. Literature comes later; spreadsheets come first. This tells you everything about *why* writing was invented: complexity of trade and administration demanded a memory that human brains alone couldn't provide.

**3. Egypt and the pyramids**

Egypt's Old Kingdom runs parallel to Mesopotamian development but produces something visually impossible to ignore. The pyramids at Giza (~2,580–2,560 BCE) are Early Bronze Age achievements, built by a state with the organizational capacity to mobilize tens of thousands of workers, feed them, house them, and coordinate logistics across decades. Whatever their spiritual meaning, they are first and foremost a demonstration of what a centralized state with agricultural surplus can accomplish.

---

### What's already in place

By the end of the Early Bronze Age, the following exist for the first time simultaneously: cities of tens of thousands, professional bureaucracies, written records, standing armies, long-distance trade, monumental architecture, and inherited political power. Every one of these was absent 2,000 years earlier. The pace of change has shifted permanently.

---

### Why it matters

The Early Bronze Age is the proof of concept for civilization. It establishes that large-scale human cooperation — beyond the tribe, beyond the village — is not only possible but enormously productive. Everything that follows is, in a sense, iteration on this original model.
