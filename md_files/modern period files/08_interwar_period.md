# Interwar Period
### World Wars · Modern Period (~1919–1939 CE)

The Interwar Period is twenty years of warning that goes unheeded — a period in which every major catastrophe of the Second World War is visible in outline, in which the decisions that might have prevented it are available and not taken, and in which the liberal democratic order, having apparently triumphed in 1918 CE, retreats across almost the entire continent within fifteen years. It is the 20th century's most instructive case study in how democracies fail.

---

### The Versailles settlement and its contradictions

The Paris Peace Conference (1919 CE) faces an impossible task: settling a war that has destroyed four empires, killed 20 million people, and left the victors' populations demanding punishment of the defeated. Wilson's Fourteen Points — self-determination, open diplomacy, a League of Nations — offer a liberal framework for a durable peace. The Versailles Treaty that emerges is a compromise that satisfies nobody: punitive enough to humiliate Germany (Article 231, the "war guilt clause," assigns sole responsibility for the war; reparations are set at 132 billion gold marks) but not punitive enough to permanently weaken it. The economist John Maynard Keynes resigns from the British delegation and publishes *The Economic Consequences of the Peace* (1919 CE), predicting accurately that the settlement's terms will produce economic instability and political resentment leading to another war.

The League of Nations — Wilson's mechanism for collective security — is fatally undermined from the start when the US Senate refuses to ratify the Versailles Treaty, keeping America out of the organization Wilson designed. Without American participation, the League lacks the authority and resources to enforce collective security against determined aggression.

### Three key developments

**1. The rise of fascism**

Fascism emerges from the specific conditions of the post-war crisis: economic instability, national humiliation, fear of communist revolution, the availability of millions of demobilized soldiers comfortable with violence, and the weakness of new democratic institutions without roots in political culture. Mussolini's March on Rome (1922 CE) brings the first fascist government to power in Italy — a template others will follow.

Hitler's National Socialist German Workers' Party exploits the specific vulnerabilities of the Weimar Republic: hyperinflation (1923 CE), the Great Depression (1929–1933 CE), the stab-in-the-back myth, and the constitutional fragility of a democracy without democrats. Hitler is appointed Chancellor in January 1933 CE through legal means — the Weimar constitution's emergency provisions and the miscalculation of conservative elites who believe they can control him. Within months, the Enabling Act gives him dictatorial powers. The Reichstag fire, the Nuremberg Laws, the Night of the Long Knives — the consolidation of totalitarian power follows a pattern that later scholarship allows us to describe with chilling precision.

**2. The Great Depression and its political consequences**

The Wall Street Crash of October 1929 CE triggers the deepest global economic contraction in the industrial era. American GDP falls by roughly 30% between 1929 and 1933 CE; unemployment reaches 25% in the United States and higher in Germany. The international gold standard transmits the contraction globally — countries tied to gold cannot reflate their economies and are forced into procyclical deflation that deepens the slump.

The Depression's political consequences are asymmetric and terrible: it destroys the credibility of liberal economic orthodoxy (balanced budgets, free trade, the gold standard) in the eyes of populations experiencing mass unemployment, and the political beneficiaries are the extremes. In the United States, Roosevelt's New Deal — a pragmatic expansion of government intervention — saves democratic capitalism. In Germany, the Depression's misery hands Hitler the electoral base that brings him to power. The contrast between these two outcomes — same shock, radically different political responses — is the 20th century's most important political science experiment.

**3. Appeasement and the road to war**

The policy of appeasement — conceding territorial demands to Hitler in the hope of satisfying his ambitions and avoiding war — is not the simple cowardice or naivety it appears in retrospect. British and French leaders in the 1930s have vivid memories of the First World War's catastrophe, face genuine military unpreparedness, and receive intelligence suggesting (incorrectly) that Germany is stronger than it is. The Munich Agreement (September 1938 CE) — giving Hitler the Sudetenland in exchange for his promise of no further territorial demands — is Chamberlain's attempt to purchase peace at the cost of Czechoslovakia's integrity.

It fails because Hitler's demands are not satisfiable — they are not primarily territorial but ideological, rooted in a worldview that requires conquest and racial domination. When Germany invades Poland on September 1, 1939 CE, Britain and France declare war. The appeasement policy's failure becomes the 20th century's defining lesson about the limits of accommodation with authoritarian aggression — a lesson subsequently over-applied in contexts where it does not fit, producing the "Munich analogy" that haunts Western foreign policy decisions for the rest of the century.

---

### Why it matters

The Interwar Period is history's most studied case of democratic failure and the preconditions for catastrophic war. Its lessons — about the dangers of punitive peace settlements, economic depression's political toxicity, the fragility of new democratic institutions, the speed with which fascism can consolidate power through legal means, and the failure of collective security — are the direct foundation of the post-World War Two order: the Marshall Plan (generous rather than punitive economic settlement), the Bretton Woods institutions (managed international economic cooperation), NATO (binding collective security), and the European project (integration to prevent nationalist competition). The post-war architects were designing against the Interwar Period's specific failures, and understanding those failures explains almost every major institution of the post-1945 world.
