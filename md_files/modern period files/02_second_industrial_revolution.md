# Second Industrial Revolution
### Industrial Era · Modern Period (~1870–1914 CE)

The Second Industrial Revolution is the First's more sophisticated, more global, and more scientifically grounded successor. Where the First Revolution was driven by practical tinkerers — Watt, Arkwright, Stephenson — working largely by trial and error, the Second is driven by the systematic application of science to industry. It produces electricity, steel, chemicals, petroleum, and the internal combustion engine — the material foundation of the 20th century — and it spreads industrialization beyond Britain to Germany, the United States, Japan, and eventually the world.

---

### The shift in leadership

By 1870 CE, Britain's industrial lead is eroding. Germany, unified in 1871 CE under Bismarck, industrializes with the deliberate backing of the state — investing in technical education, scientific research institutes, and strategic industries in ways that Victorian Britain's laissez-faire ideology resists. By 1900 CE, Germany produces more steel than Britain and leads the world in chemicals and electrical equipment. The United States, with its vast internal market, abundant resources, and waves of immigrant labor, industrializes even faster: by 1900 CE American GDP surpasses Britain's, and American steel production surpasses that of Britain and Germany combined.

This shift in industrial leadership is simultaneously a shift in geopolitical weight — with consequences that will become catastrophically apparent in 1914 CE.

---

### Three key developments

**1. Steel, electricity, and chemicals**

The Bessemer process (1856 CE) and open-hearth furnace (1860s CE) allow steel — stronger, more flexible, and more workable than iron — to be produced cheaply at industrial scale for the first time. Steel makes possible skyscrapers, suspension bridges, ocean liners, and the railway networks that knit industrial economies together. The Brooklyn Bridge (completed 1883 CE), the Eiffel Tower (1889 CE), and the first skyscrapers of Chicago are all Second Industrial Revolution steel structures — demonstrations of what the new material makes architecturally possible.

Electricity transforms the industrial economy's energy infrastructure. Edison's incandescent bulb (1879 CE) and Pearl Street Station in New York (1882 CE — the world's first commercial electrical grid) begin the transition from gas lighting to electric light. Tesla's alternating current system, backed by Westinghouse against Edison's direct current, wins the "war of currents" by the 1890s CE and enables electricity to be transmitted over long distances — powering not just lights but motors, replacing steam power in factories and eventually enabling the electric grid that makes the modern world possible.

The German chemical industry produces synthetic dyes (aniline dyes, from the 1850s CE), fertilizers (the Haber-Bosch process for synthetic nitrogen fixation, perfected 1909 CE — arguably the most important chemical invention in history, enabling the agricultural yields that support the 20th century's population growth), and pharmaceuticals (aspirin, 1899 CE; the first antibiotics follow in the 20th century). Science and industry are now inseparable.

**2. The internal combustion engine and petroleum**

The internal combustion engine, developed through the 1860s–80s CE by Otto, Daimler, and Benz, uses petroleum rather than coal as its energy source. Karl Benz's Patent-Motorwagen (1885 CE) is the first true automobile. Henry Ford's Model T (1908 CE) and the moving assembly line at Highland Park (1913 CE) make the automobile accessible to middle-class Americans and inaugurate mass consumer manufacturing — the production logic that will define 20th-century capitalism.

Petroleum goes from a curiosity to the world's most strategically important commodity within a generation. John D. Rockefeller's Standard Oil, controlling roughly 90% of US refining capacity by the 1880s CE, is the period's most powerful corporation and the template for the industrial monopoly — forcing the first major antitrust legislation (the Sherman Act, 1890 CE) and eventually the Supreme Court's landmark 1911 CE breakup order. The oil economy's geopolitical consequences — the scramble for Middle Eastern concessions, the centrality of petroleum to 20th-century warfare — begin here.

**3. The telegraph and the first globalization**

The electric telegraph, commercialized in the 1840s CE, compresses communication time from weeks to minutes across continental distances. The transatlantic telegraph cable (successfully laid 1866 CE) makes near-instantaneous communication between Europe and North America possible. Financial markets, commodity prices, and political news now travel at the speed of electricity rather than the speed of ships.

This communications revolution is the infrastructure of the period's first globalization: the integration of world markets in goods, capital, and labor between roughly 1870 and 1914 CE that produces levels of economic interdependence not seen again until the late 20th century. Capital flows from Britain to Argentina, the United States, Australia, and India in quantities that fund railways, ranches, and mines. Tens of millions of Europeans emigrate to the Americas and Australia. Food prices in London are determined by harvests in Kansas. The world is, by 1914 CE, more economically integrated than it will be again for sixty years — a fact that makes the catastrophe of that year's war all the more destructive.

---

### Why it matters

The Second Industrial Revolution establishes the material infrastructure of the 20th century: steel cities, electrical grids, petroleum-powered transport, chemical agriculture, mass consumer manufacturing, and global financial integration. It also shifts the balance of industrial power decisively from Britain to Germany and the United States — creating the geopolitical tensions that produce the First World War. And it demonstrates, through the Haber-Bosch process alone, that industrial science can literally reshape the biological productivity of the planet: roughly half of the nitrogen in the human body today passes through synthetic fertilizer. The boundary between the human-made and the natural world, already blurring, becomes increasingly difficult to locate.
