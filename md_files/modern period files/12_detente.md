# Détente
### Cold War · Modern Period (~1969–1979 CE)

Détente — the French word for "relaxation" or "easing" — is the Cold War's most sustained attempt at managed coexistence: the recognition by both superpowers that the ideological competition for world dominance must be conducted within limits that prevent mutual annihilation, and that economic and strategic interests can sometimes override ideological hostility. It is neither peace nor cold war at its coldest — it is a sophisticated, cynical, and partially successful attempt to make a dangerous competition less dangerous, conducted by two of the 20th century's most unsentimental realists.

---

### Nixon, Kissinger, and the realpolitik turn

Richard Nixon and Henry Kissinger bring to American foreign policy an explicitly realist framework — the primacy of strategic interest over ideological consistency, the manipulation of the triangular relationship between the United States, the Soviet Union, and China, and the acceptance of Soviet power as a permanent fact requiring management rather than elimination. This is not idealism; it is the opposite. Kissinger's worldview, shaped by his study of Metternich and the Concert of Europe, treats the international system as a balance of power that skilled diplomacy can stabilize.

Nixon's opening to China (1972 CE) — the most dramatic diplomatic reversal of the Cold War — exploits the Sino-Soviet split (the ideological and territorial conflict between China and the Soviet Union that has fractured the communist world since the late 1950s) to create a triangular balance. By normalizing relations with China, Nixon gives the Soviet Union an additional strategic concern on its eastern flank, increasing American leverage in superpower negotiations. The logic is cold and effective.

### Three key developments

**1. SALT and arms control**

The Strategic Arms Limitation Talks produce SALT I (1972 CE) and SALT II (negotiated 1979 CE, never ratified) — the first agreements to cap the growth of nuclear arsenals. SALT I freezes the number of strategic nuclear missile launchers and limits anti-ballistic missile systems — the key insight being that ABM systems destabilize deterrence by creating the possibility of a first strike without retaliation. Limiting defenses paradoxically increases stability.

These agreements don't reduce nuclear arsenals — both sides continue developing more sophisticated weapons within the agreed numerical limits — but they establish the principle that arms control is possible and that verification (through "national technical means" — satellites and intelligence) can make agreements workable without on-site inspection. The SALT process creates the institutional habits and diplomatic relationships that the more ambitious agreements of the late Cold War — INF Treaty, START — build upon.

**2. Helsinki and the human rights contradiction**

The Helsinki Accords (1975 CE), signed by 35 countries including the Soviet Union, are the Détente period's most consequential and least expected product. The agreement has three "baskets": security arrangements recognizing post-war European borders (what the Soviets want), economic cooperation (what both sides want), and human rights commitments — freedom of thought, conscience, religion, and information (what the West wants as a price for the rest).

The Soviets sign the human rights provisions expecting them to be meaningless — words on paper with no enforcement mechanism. They miscalculate. Helsinki Watch groups spring up across Eastern Europe, organized around the Soviet Union's own signature, documenting human rights violations and creating networks of dissidents with international legitimacy. Václav Havel in Czechoslovakia, Andrei Sakharov in the Soviet Union, and their counterparts across the Eastern bloc use the Helsinki commitments as a platform. The moral delegitimization of Soviet rule — central to its eventual collapse — owes more to the Helsinki process than to any military or economic pressure.

**3. The limits of Détente: Angola, Afghanistan, and collapse**

Détente's architects assume it will moderate Soviet behavior globally. It doesn't. The Soviet Union continues proxy competition in the developing world — Angola (1975 CE), Ethiopia (1977 CE) — while accepting arms control in Europe. Kissinger's response is to treat these as manageable within the overall framework; American hawks argue that Détente is one-sided appeasement.

The Soviet invasion of Afghanistan in December 1979 CE ends Détente conclusively. Carter, who has staked his foreign policy on human rights and arms control, responds with the Carter Doctrine (pledging to defend Persian Gulf access), the Olympic boycott, grain embargoes, and — most consequentially — the covert arming of Afghan mujahideen resistance fighters through the CIA. The Afghanistan intervention will cost the Soviet Union roughly 15,000 lives and enormous treasure over nine years, contributing to the economic exhaustion that accelerates its collapse. It will also arm and train the jihadist networks that produce the Taliban and, eventually, al-Qaeda — Détente's most bitter unintended consequence.

---

### Why it matters

Détente matters as a study in the possibilities and limits of great-power competition management. It demonstrates that ideological enemies can cooperate on specific issues when strategic interests converge — arms control, trade, crisis prevention — without resolving underlying conflicts. The Helsinki process shows that human rights commitments extracted as diplomatic concessions can have unexpected and transformative effects. And its collapse over Afghanistan demonstrates that managed competition requires both sides to accept the same boundaries — when one side decides the rules don't apply to its vital interests, the framework fails. These lessons — about what arms control can and cannot achieve, about the power of human rights norms, about the fragility of great-power management frameworks — are as relevant to contemporary geopolitics as they were in 1975.
