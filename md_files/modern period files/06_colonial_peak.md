# Colonial Peak
### Age of Imperialism · Modern Period (~1900–1939 CE)

The Colonial Peak is the imperial system at maximum territorial extent and minimum legitimacy simultaneously. By 1920 CE, European empires control roughly 85% of the Earth's land surface — the highest point ever reached. They also face, for the first time, organized intellectual and political opposition that uses the empires' own stated values against them: if liberty and self-determination are universal principles (as the Allied powers claim in fighting the First World War), why do they not apply to colonized peoples? The colonial peak is the moment before the fall, and the fall, when it comes, is rapid.

---

### The system at full extension

The British Empire at its 1920 CE peak encompasses roughly 35 million square kilometers and 458 million people — about a quarter of the world's population and land area. The French Empire is the second largest, extending across North and West Africa, Indochina, Madagascar, and the Pacific. The Dutch hold Indonesia, the Belgians the Congo, the Portuguese Mozambique and Angola, the Italians Libya and Somalia. The Japanese Empire, the only non-Western imperial power, controls Korea, Taiwan, and Manchuria, with ambitions toward China.

This territorial extent is maintained by a combination of colonial administrators (shockingly few relative to the populations they govern), local collaborators, selective violence, and the credible threat of more. The British Raj governs 300 million Indians with roughly 1,000 ICS officers. The maintenance of empire requires constant calibration of violence — enough to demonstrate consequences for resistance, not so much as to generate unmanageable rebellion.

---

### Three key developments

**1. The First World War and anti-colonial mobilization**

The First World War is simultaneously the imperial system's greatest demonstration of reach and the beginning of its delegitimization. Roughly 4 million Africans and Asians serve in European armies — French Senegalese troops, Indian Army soldiers, Anzacs from Australia and New Zealand — fighting for empires whose promises of equality and self-determination they will invoke afterward. Woodrow Wilson's Fourteen Points (1918 CE), proclaiming the principle of national self-determination, are heard by colonial subjects as applying to them — an interpretation Wilson emphatically does not intend but cannot logically refute.

Ho Chi Minh attends the Paris Peace Conference in 1919 CE with a petition for Vietnamese self-determination, in Western suit and hat, citing Wilson's principles. He is ignored. He leaves for Moscow. The story encapsulates how the First World War radicalizes the generation that will lead anti-colonial movements after the Second.

**2. Anti-colonial movements and their intellectual foundations**

The interwar period produces the intellectual and organizational infrastructure of decolonization. Gandhi's non-cooperation movement (1920–1922 CE) and Salt March (1930 CE) demonstrate that mass civil disobedience can impose political and economic costs on colonial rule. The Indian National Congress, founded 1885 CE as a moderate reform organization, transforms under Gandhi and Nehru into a mass movement demanding independence.

Pan-Africanism — the political and cultural movement asserting African solidarity and dignity — develops in the diaspora (W.E.B. Du Bois, Marcus Garvey) and on the continent, challenging the racial hierarchy that justifies colonial rule. Aimé Césaire's *Negritude* movement in the French Caribbean and Africa asserts African cultural value against colonial denigration. Frantz Fanon will later theorize the psychological dimensions of colonial subjugation and anti-colonial resistance. The intellectual case for colonialism — never philosophically consistent — collapses under this sustained critique from within the empires' own stated values.

**3. The mandate system and the Middle East's founding wounds**

The post-World War One settlement produces the mandate system — the League of Nations' framework for distributing former German and Ottoman territories to Britain and France as "mandates" to be governed in trust for eventual self-determination. In practice it is colonialism with a fig leaf of international legitimacy. The British and French mandates in the Middle East — drawing the borders of Iraq, Syria, Lebanon, Palestine, and Transjordan — are among history's most consequential acts of cartographic violence.

The Sykes-Picot Agreement (1916 CE) divides the Arab world between British and French spheres of influence, simultaneously with the Balfour Declaration (1917 CE) promising a Jewish homeland in Palestine — commitments made to different parties that are fundamentally incompatible. The borders drawn in this period — lumping Kurds, Sunni Arabs, and Shia Arabs into Iraq; separating Palestinians between British mandate and eventual Israeli state; creating Lebanon's fragile multi-confessional formula — generate conflicts that are still unresolved. The Middle East's contemporary crises are, in substantial part, the Colonial Peak's unfinished business.

---

### Why it matters

The Colonial Peak matters because it is the direct antecedent of the contemporary world's political geography, economic hierarchies, and unresolved conflicts. The borders of the Middle East, the tensions in South Asia between India and Pakistan, the political instability of post-colonial African states, the economic relationships between former metropoles and their former colonies — all are legacies of decisions made at the imperial system's peak. Decolonization dismantles the formal political structure of empire but cannot instantly undo the economic dependencies, political boundaries, and social hierarchies that decades or centuries of colonial rule have created. The colonial peak's long shadow is the 21st century's most persistent historical inheritance.
