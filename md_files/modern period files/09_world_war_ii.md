# World War II
### World Wars · Modern Period (~1939–1945 CE)

The Second World War is the largest and most destructive conflict in human history — killing between 70 and 85 million people, roughly 3% of the world's population, the majority of them civilians. It is simultaneously a continuation of the First World War's unfinished business, an ideological struggle between fascism, communism, and liberal democracy, a war of racial extermination conducted by the Nazi state, and the event that definitively ends European dominance of world affairs and inaugurates the American century. Everything about the post-war world — the United Nations, NATO, the Cold War, decolonization, Israel, the nuclear age — is shaped by what happens between 1939 and 1945 CE.

---

### The scale and simultaneity

The war's defining characteristic is its simultaneity across multiple theaters — Europe, North Africa, the Eastern Front, the Pacific, the Atlantic, the China-Burma-India theater — each with its own strategic logic, and all interconnected. The Eastern Front alone — the war between Germany and the Soviet Union — is the largest military confrontation in history: roughly 30 million Soviet deaths, 6 million German deaths, and battles of a scale (Kursk, 1943 CE, involved approximately 6,000 tanks) that dwarfs anything in the Western theater. The Eastern Front decides the European war's outcome; D-Day (June 6, 1944 CE) is strategically important but arrives when Germany is already being ground down by Soviet force.

---

### Three key developments

**1. The Holocaust**

The Holocaust — the systematic murder of approximately six million Jews and the genocide of Roma, disabled people, homosexuals, Soviet POWs, and others — is the 20th century's defining moral catastrophe. It is not an accidental byproduct of the war but its deliberate accompaniment: the Nazi state's racial ideology, articulated in *Mein Kampf* and the Nuremberg Laws, requires the physical elimination of the Jewish people as an ideological imperative.

The process escalates through stages: legal discrimination, expropriation, forced emigration, ghettoization, mass shootings by the *Einsatzgruppen* (mobile killing units) following the invasion of the Soviet Union in 1941 CE, and finally the industrial extermination centers — Auschwitz-Birkenau, Treblinka, Sobibor, Belzec — where approximately 3 million people are murdered by gas. The bureaucratic normality of the process — ordinary civil servants administering mass murder, railway timetables coordinating deportations, companies tendering for crematorium contracts — is what Hannah Arendt means by the "banality of evil": evil requiring not monsters but ordinary people following orders within an institutional system that normalizes atrocity.

The Holocaust produces the Nuremberg Trials (1945–1946 CE) — the first international criminal tribunal for crimes against humanity — and eventually the 1948 Genocide Convention and Universal Declaration of Human Rights. It is the founding trauma of the international human rights order.

**2. Total war and the mobilization of economies**

The Second World War is a competition between industrial economies operating under total war mobilization — and the side that can produce more wins. American war production is the decisive factor: 300,000 aircraft, 100,000 tanks, 71,000 naval vessels, 2.4 million trucks, and 15 million rifles. The Liberty Ship program produces a complete cargo vessel every 42 days by 1943 CE. This industrial output is simply beyond Germany or Japan's capacity to match — the outcome is determined as much in American factories as on the battlefield.

The Soviet Union's industrial mobilization is equally remarkable under far worse conditions: the transfer of entire factory complexes east of the Urals in 1941–42 CE, disassembled, transported on railcars, and reassembled in Siberia beyond German air range, producing the tanks and guns that stop and eventually reverse the German advance. The Soviet economy, devastated by the German invasion, still outproduces Germany in armaments by 1943 CE.

**3. The atomic bomb and the new world**

The Manhattan Project — the American, British, and Canadian program to develop nuclear weapons, drawing on refugee physicists from Nazi Europe (Einstein, Fermi, Szilard, Teller, Oppenheimer) — produces the first nuclear test at Trinity, New Mexico, in July 1945 CE. The bombs dropped on Hiroshima (August 6) and Nagasaki (August 9) kill approximately 130,000–226,000 people immediately, with tens of thousands more dying from radiation in subsequent months, and force Japan's surrender on August 15, 1945 CE.

The decision to use atomic weapons — bypassing an invasion of Japan projected to cost hundreds of thousands of Allied and millions of Japanese lives — remains the most debated strategic decision of the 20th century. What is not debated is the consequence: the atomic bomb changes the nature of warfare and international relations permanently. The arms race that follows, the doctrine of mutually assured destruction, the proliferation debates, the near-misses of the Cold War — all proceed from August 1945 CE. The world lives in the atomic age's shadow from that point forward.

---

### Why it matters

The Second World War matters as both end and beginning. It ends European dominance of world affairs — Britain and France emerge victorious but exhausted, economically dependent on the United States, unable to maintain their empires. It ends fascism as a viable governing ideology (temporarily, at least). And it ends the pre-nuclear age's assumptions about warfare as a tool of policy. As a beginning, it inaugurates the American-dominated international order, the Cold War, the decolonization wave, the nuclear deterrence framework, and the international institutions — the UN, the World Bank, the IMF, the GATT/WTO — designed to prevent its recurrence. The post-war world is, in its institutions and assumptions, an extended argument about what caused the Second World War and how to prevent the next one.
