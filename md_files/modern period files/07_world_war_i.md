# World War I
### World Wars · Modern Period (~1914–1918 CE)

The First World War is the 20th century's original catastrophe — the event from which most of the century's subsequent disasters flow. It kills approximately 20 million people, destroys four empires, produces the conditions for the Russian Revolution, plants the seeds of fascism and the Second World War, and permanently shatters the confidence of European civilization in its own progressive narrative. The men who fought it went to war expecting the cavalry charges and decisive battles of 19th-century warfare. They encountered something no one had imagined: industrial killing on an industrial scale, in a stalemate that lasted four years and consumed a generation.

---

### How it starts: structure and spark

The war's causes are still debated, but the structural explanation is more important than the spark. By 1914 CE, Europe has assembled a set of conditions that make a large war extremely likely: two heavily armed alliance systems (the Triple Entente of France, Russia, and Britain versus the Triple Alliance of Germany, Austria-Hungary, and Italy) facing each other across contested borders; a naval arms race between Britain and Germany generating mutual suspicion; mobilization plans — particularly Germany's Schlieffen Plan — that, once triggered, run on a timetable no political decision can easily reverse; nationalist tensions across the multinational Austro-Hungarian and Ottoman empires; and decades of colonial competition creating a climate of zero-sum international competition.

The spark is the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria-Hungary by the Bosnian Serb nationalist Gavrilo Princip in Sarajevo on June 28, 1914 CE. Within six weeks, the alliance system has converted a Balkan dispute into a continental war. The July Crisis — the five weeks of diplomatic maneuvering between the assassination and the outbreak of war — is one of history's most studied examples of how rational actors in a poorly designed system can produce catastrophic outcomes none of them individually wants.

---

### Three key developments

**1. The Western Front and the failure of movement**

The Schlieffen Plan — Germany's strategy for avoiding a two-front war by rapidly defeating France through Belgium before turning to face Russia — fails in the first weeks. The French and British stop the German advance at the Battle of the Marne (September 1914 CE), and both sides dig in. The result is 700 kilometers of trenches stretching from the Swiss border to the English Channel — a defensive system that neither side can break through for four years.

The technology of 1914 CE enormously favors the defender over the attacker. Machine guns, barbed wire, and artillery can devastate attacking infantry crossing open ground. The communications technology to coordinate a breakthrough — radio is too bulky and unreliable; telephone wire breaks under bombardment — doesn't exist. The result is a recurring pattern: massive artillery bombardment (meant to destroy defenses and cut wire), followed by infantry assault, followed by catastrophic casualties for minimal territorial gain. The Battle of the Somme (July–November 1916 CE) produces 57,470 British casualties on its first day — still the bloodiest day in British military history — for an advance of a few kilometers.

**2. Total war and the home front**

The First World War industrializes not just killing but national mobilization. Governments assume control of economies, directing industrial production toward the war effort, rationing food and raw materials, conscripting labor as well as soldiers. Women enter industrial employment at scale for the first time — a transformation that accelerates the suffrage movements that produce women's voting rights in Britain (1918 CE), Germany (1919 CE), and the United States (1920 CE).

The war requires unprecedented propaganda to sustain public support for what is, by 1916 CE, obviously not the short victorious war of popular expectation. Governments deploy mass communication — posters, film, censored press — to maintain the narrative of noble sacrifice. The gap between this narrative and the reality of the trenches, documented by the war poets — Wilfred Owen, Siegfried Sassoon — becomes the defining cultural rupture of the war: the loss of faith in authority, in progress, and in the language of heroism that previous generations had taken for granted.

**3. 1917: the turning year**

1917 CE is the war's hinge. In March, the Russian Revolution overthrows the Tsar; in November, the Bolshevik Revolution brings Lenin to power and eventually takes Russia out of the war, freeing German forces for the Western Front. In April, the United States enters the war — tipped by Germany's resumption of unrestricted submarine warfare and the Zimmermann Telegram (Germany's proposal that Mexico attack the United States in exchange for territorial concessions). American industrial and manpower resources are decisive: the war's outcome is no longer in doubt once American troops arrive in force in 1918 CE.

The German spring offensives of 1918 CE — the last desperate attempt to win before American forces tip the balance — achieve tactical success but strategic failure. By August the Allied counter-offensive is irreversible. The Armistice is signed on November 11, 1918 CE. The German army has not been defeated on the field — it retreats in good order — a fact that right-wing nationalists will exploit as the "stab in the back" myth, blaming Germany's defeat on domestic betrayal rather than military failure. This myth will be central to Hitler's political appeal.

---

### Why it matters

The First World War matters because it is the 20th century's inaugural catastrophe — the event that makes everything else more likely. The Russian Revolution produces the Soviet Union, the Cold War, and 70 years of communist governance over a third of humanity. The Versailles settlement's punitive terms produce the economic instability and nationalist resentment that bring Hitler to power. The destruction of the Ottoman Empire produces the Middle East borders that generate conflicts to this day. The loss of an entire generation's confidence in European civilization produces the cultural modernism — Eliot's *Waste Land*, Joyce's *Ulysses*, Picasso's fragmented forms — that is the 20th century's dominant artistic response to a world that has lost its certainties. Almost nothing that happens afterward can be understood without understanding what happened between 1914 and 1918.
