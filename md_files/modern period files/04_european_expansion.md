# European Expansion
### Age of Imperialism · Modern Period (~1815–1880 CE)

European Expansion in the 19th century is the Age of Exploration's colonial project operating at a new scale and with new tools. The same impulses — commercial profit, strategic competition, missionary ambition, and genuine curiosity — now operate with industrial technology, steam-powered gunboats, repeating rifles, and telegraph communications that make the power differential between European states and non-European societies more extreme than at any previous point in history. The result is the most rapid territorial conquest in human history, covering most of the globe within a single century.

---

### The new imperialism's tools

What makes 19th-century imperialism different from its Early Modern predecessor is the technology gap. Quinine, developed as a prophylactic against malaria in the 1850s CE, opens the African interior — previously lethal to Europeans — to sustained penetration. The steam-powered riverboat can move troops and supplies up rivers that previously formed natural barriers. The Maxim gun (1884 CE), the world's first self-powered machine gun, fires 500 rounds per minute — a rate of fire that makes small European forces capable of defeating mass armies. At the Battle of Omdurman (1898 CE), roughly 8,000 British and Egyptian troops kill approximately 10,000–12,000 Sudanese Mahdist warriors in a morning, losing 47 men. Kipling's verse captures the logic with brutal economy: "Whatever happens, we have got / The Maxim gun, and they have not."

The ideological justification is equally new. Social Darwinism — the misapplication of Darwin's evolutionary theory to human societies — provides a pseudo-scientific framework for ranking races and civilizations on a hierarchy with white Europeans at the top. The "civilizing mission" — the claim that European conquest benefits subject peoples by bringing Christianity, commerce, and civilization — is partly sincere self-deception and partly deliberate propaganda, believed most fervently by those who profit from it least.

---

### Three key developments

**1. The consolidation of British India**

Britain's Indian empire evolves from the East India Company's commercial presence into direct Crown rule through a series of wars and annexations across the first half of the 19th century. The Indian Rebellion of 1857 CE — the most serious challenge to British rule, beginning as a mutiny in the Bengal Army and spreading into a broad popular uprising — is suppressed with extreme violence and leads directly to the abolition of the East India Company and the formal declaration of British sovereignty over India (the Raj) in 1858 CE.

The Raj at its height governs 300 million people through approximately 1,000 British Indian Civil Service officers — a ratio of administration that is only possible through the collaboration of Indian elites, the selective use of violence, and the constant threat of more. The economic consequences are contested but substantial: British deindustrialization of Indian textile production (destroying the hand-loom weaving industry that had made India a global textile exporter), extraction of agricultural surplus during famines, and the construction of a railway network that primarily serves British commercial and military interests.

**2. The Ottoman Empire and the Eastern Question**

The 19th century sees the slow disintegration of the Ottoman Empire — once the dominant power from the Balkans to the Persian Gulf — under European pressure. The "Eastern Question" — what to do about the collapsing Ottoman state and who gets its territories — is the central diplomatic problem of the century, generating the Crimean War (1853–1856 CE), the Russo-Turkish War (1877–1878 CE), and a series of Balkan crises that eventually produce the spark for the First World War.

European powers carve Ottoman territories into spheres of influence — France in North Africa, Britain in Egypt and the Gulf, Russia pressing toward the Bosphorus and Central Asia. The nationalist movements that the French Revolution has seeded across Europe now activate among Ottoman subject peoples: Greeks (independent 1821–1829 CE), Romanians, Serbs, Bulgarians, Armenians — each pursuing national self-determination in a context where European powers cynically support or oppose their causes based on strategic interest rather than principle.

**3. Latin America and informal empire**

European expansion in the 19th century is not solely territorial. Britain, the period's dominant financial and commercial power, exercises what historians call "informal empire" across Latin America — using financial leverage, commercial treaties, and occasional military intervention to secure favorable conditions for British trade and investment without the administrative costs of formal colonialism. Argentina, Brazil, and Chile are formally independent states but their railways, banks, and export sectors are largely British-owned and British-financed.

The United States develops its own version of informal imperialism in the Western Hemisphere, articulated by the Monroe Doctrine (1823 CE) as opposition to European colonialism — but practiced, increasingly through the century, as American domination of Central America and the Caribbean. The transition from European to American informal dominance in Latin America is gradual through the 19th century and complete by the early 20th.

---

### Why it matters

European Expansion in this phase establishes the global hierarchy of the modern world: the industrial core (Europe, North America, eventually Japan) and the extractive periphery (Africa, Asia, Latin America) whose relationship is structured by the colonial encounter. The economic development theory of the 20th century — why some countries are rich and others poor — is in large part an argument about what the 19th-century imperial encounter did to the trajectories of different societies. The political map of the Middle East, Africa, and South Asia, drawn largely by European imperial decisions, generates conflicts that are still active today. European Expansion is not ancient history — its consequences are the present's most contested inheritances.
