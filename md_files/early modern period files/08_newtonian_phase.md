# Newtonian Phase
### Scientific Revolution · Early Modern Period (~1610–1720 CE)

The Newtonian Phase is where the Scientific Revolution delivers its proof of concept — where the new methods of observation, experiment, and mathematical reasoning produce results so powerful and so practically useful that their authority becomes impossible to deny. It opens with Galileo pointing a telescope at the sky and closes with Newton's synthesis of celestial and terrestrial mechanics into a single mathematical framework. In between, it invents experimental science, transforms medicine, and establishes the institutional infrastructure through which modern science still operates.

---

### Galileo and the telescope

Galileo Galilei (~1564–1642 CE) does not invent the telescope, but in 1609–1610 CE he is the first to systematically point one at the sky and understand what he sees. The *Siderius Nuncius* (1610 CE) reports observations that directly contradict the Aristotelian-Ptolemaic universe: the Moon has mountains and craters, meaning celestial matter is not perfectly smooth; Jupiter has four moons orbiting it, meaning not everything orbits Earth; Venus shows phases like the Moon, meaning it orbits the Sun. The evidence is now not mathematical argument but visible, repeatable observation.

Galileo's conflict with the Church — culminating in his 1633 CE trial and forced recantation — is the period's most famous science-religion confrontation. What makes it historically significant is not the drama but the principle it establishes: empirical evidence can and should override institutional authority in determining facts about the physical world. Galileo is condemned; the Earth continues to orbit the Sun.

His contributions extend beyond astronomy. His experiments with falling bodies (the legendary — probably apocryphal — dropping of cannonballs from the Leaning Tower of Pisa, but more importantly his mathematical analysis of inclined plane experiments) demonstrate that objects fall at the same rate regardless of mass, that motion is the natural state rather than requiring constant force, and that physical phenomena can be described in precise mathematical language. This is the methodological foundation of classical mechanics.

---

### Three key developments

**1. The experimental method institutionalized**

Francis Bacon's *Novum Organum* (1620 CE) provides the philosophical framework: knowledge should be built inductively from systematic experiment and observation, not deduced from first principles. This sounds obvious now; in 1620 it is a radical challenge to the Aristotelian tradition that had dominated intellectual life for two thousand years. Bacon's influence on subsequent scientific practice is partly methodological and partly institutional — he envisions science as a collaborative, cumulative, socially organized enterprise rather than individual genius.

The Royal Society of London (founded 1660 CE) and the French Académie des Sciences (founded 1666 CE) are Bacon's vision institutionalized: organizations dedicated to experimental inquiry, the sharing of results, and the replication and criticism of findings. The peer review process — crude and informal at first — begins here. Science stops being what individual geniuses do alone and becomes what organized communities of researchers do together. This institutional change is as important as any specific discovery.

**2. Newton's synthesis**

Isaac Newton's *Principia Mathematica* (1687 CE) is arguably the single most important scientific publication in history. In it, Newton demonstrates that the same mathematical law — universal gravitation — governs both the falling of an apple and the orbit of the Moon, both the trajectory of a cannonball and the path of a comet. The celestial and terrestrial realms, separated by two thousand years of Aristotelian physics, are unified under a single set of equations.

Newton's three laws of motion provide the foundation for classical mechanics that will remain sufficient for virtually all practical purposes until Einstein. His development of calculus (simultaneously and independently invented by Leibniz) provides the mathematical tools to analyze continuous change. The universe of the *Principia* is a clockwork mechanism governed by precise, universal mathematical laws — the "Newtonian worldview" that will dominate Western science and philosophy for the next two centuries.

**3. The revolution in medicine and biology**

The Scientific Revolution transforms medicine alongside physics. Andreas Vesalius's *De Humani Corporis Fabrica* (1543 CE — the same year as Copernicus) corrects Galen's anatomy through systematic dissection, establishing that knowledge of the human body requires looking at actual bodies rather than reading ancient texts. William Harvey demonstrates in *De Motu Cordis* (1628 CE) that blood circulates continuously through the body, powered by the heart as a pump — overturning the 1,400-year-old Galenic model of blood being produced in the liver and consumed by the body. Antonie van Leeuwenhoek's microscopic observations in the 1670s reveal a world of microorganisms completely invisible to the naked eye — laying the groundwork, two centuries later, for germ theory.

---

### Why it matters

The Newtonian Phase establishes the template for how modern science works: systematic experiment, mathematical description of natural laws, peer institution review, and the principle that no authority — ancient, religious, or political — can override empirical evidence about the physical world. More immediately, it produces the Newtonian universe — a lawful, comprehensible, mathematically describable cosmos — that is the precondition for the Enlightenment's confidence that the same rational methods applied to the natural world can be applied to society, government, and human nature. The Enlightenment is, in large part, the project of applying Newton's methods to everything else.
