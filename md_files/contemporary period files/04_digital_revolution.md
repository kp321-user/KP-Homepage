# Digital Revolution
### Globalization · Contemporary (~1970–2000 CE)

The Digital Revolution is the most rapid and comprehensive transformation of the material basis of human communication, commerce, and cognition since the printing press — and it happens in roughly thirty years rather than three centuries. It converts the analog world of physical media, face-to-face transactions, and local knowledge into a digital world of networked information, global markets, and computational intelligence. Everything it touches — manufacturing, finance, media, warfare, social organization, politics — is transformed, usually faster than institutions can adapt. We are still in the middle of this transformation and cannot yet see its end.

---

### The technological cascade

The Digital Revolution is not a single invention but a cascade of mutually enabling technologies. The transistor (1947 CE) enables solid-state electronics; the integrated circuit (1958–1959 CE) puts thousands of transistors on a chip; Moore's Law (Gordon Moore's 1965 CE observation that chip transistor density doubles roughly every two years) describes an exponential improvement trajectory that holds for fifty years. The microprocessor (Intel 4004, 1971 CE) puts a complete CPU on a single chip, enabling the personal computer. The internet (ARPANET, 1969 CE, opening to commercial use in the 1990s CE) connects computers into a global network. The World Wide Web (Tim Berners-Lee, 1991 CE) makes that network navigable by non-specialists. Each advance enables the next; the cumulative effect is transformative at a pace no previous technology has achieved.

---

### Three key developments

**1. The personal computer and the democratization of computation**

The Apple II (1977 CE) and IBM PC (1981 CE) bring computing from institutional mainframes to individual desks. The spreadsheet (VisiCalc, 1979 CE) is the killer application that drives early adoption — accountants and business analysts who previously spent days on manual calculations can now do them in minutes. Word processing eliminates the typewriter. Desktop publishing eliminates the typesetter. Each application displaces an existing profession while creating new ones, at a pace that labor markets struggle to absorb.

The personal computer also begins the transformation of knowledge work. The ability to store, search, copy, and manipulate information digitally changes what it means to do intellectual labor — a researcher who previously spent weeks in libraries can now search digitized databases in hours; a writer who previously retyped entire manuscripts to make revisions can now edit with a keystroke. The productivity implications are enormous and unevenly distributed: knowledge workers gain enormous leverage from digital tools; routine clerical and administrative workers find their jobs automated away.

**2. The internet and the collapse of information geography**

The internet's commercialization in the mid-1990s CE — enabled by the World Wide Web, the graphical web browser (Mosaic/Netscape, 1993–1994 CE), and the rapid expansion of internet service providers — produces a transformation in information access with no historical precedent except the printing press. In 1990 CE, accessing information requires physical proximity to a library, an expert, or a physical medium. By 2000 CE, a networked computer can access more information than any physical library ever held.

The economic consequences are immediate and disruptive. E-commerce (Amazon, 1994 CE; eBay, 1995 CE) creates retail channels that bypass physical stores. Online classified advertising destroys the newspaper industry's most profitable revenue stream within a decade. Music, journalism, software, and film distribution are all transformed as digital copies eliminates the physical scarcity on which their business models depend. The internet does not merely change how existing industries operate — it renders some of them economically nonviable.

**3. Finance, globalization, and the digital economy**

The Digital Revolution enables the financial globalization of the 1980s–1990s CE by providing the computational infrastructure needed to execute, clear, and manage complex financial transactions at global scale and millisecond speed. Derivatives markets — whose complexity requires computers to price and manage — grow from near-zero in the 1970s to hundreds of trillions of dollars in notional value by 2000 CE. High-frequency trading, algorithmic risk management, and global currency speculation are all digital-era phenomena, enabled by computing power and network connectivity.

This financial digitization connects capital markets globally in ways that amplify both efficiency and fragility. Capital can move between countries in milliseconds, seeking the highest return — an efficiency that disciplines governments to maintain investor-friendly policies but also makes financial crises transmit globally at digital speed. The Asian financial crisis (1997–1998 CE), the Russian default (1998 CE), and the LTCM collapse (1998 CE) are early demonstrations of how digital financial integration creates systemic risks that no individual actor can foresee or manage.

---

### Why it matters

The Digital Revolution matters because it is still happening — we are not yet able to see its full consequences with the clarity that historical distance provides. What is clear is that it represents the most thorough transformation of the material basis of human communication and economic activity since the Industrial Revolution, occurring roughly ten times faster. Its political consequences — the disruption of established media, the creation of new platforms for organizing and radicalization, the surveillance capabilities it places in the hands of both states and corporations — are still unfolding. Its economic consequences — the winner-take-all dynamics of platform markets, the automation of routine cognitive work, the geographic concentration of high-value employment — are reshaping the social foundations of democratic politics in ways that will occupy the 21st century's political agenda for decades.
