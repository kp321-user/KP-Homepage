# Global Integration
### Globalization · Contemporary (~1980–2020 CE)

Global Integration is the economic and institutional process by which the world's separate national economies become progressively more interdependent — through trade, capital flows, migration, and the multinational corporations that organize production across borders. It is the most significant restructuring of the global economy since the colonial era, and like the colonial era it produces extraordinary aggregate gains unevenly distributed in ways that generate political backlash. By the 2010s CE, that backlash has become the defining force in the politics of every major economy.

---

### The second globalization

The first globalization ran roughly from 1870 to 1914 CE, ended abruptly by the First World War, and was definitively reversed by the protectionism of the interwar period. The second globalization begins with the trade liberalization of the post-war period and accelerates dramatically from the 1980s CE through a specific combination of policy choices and technological enablers. The Reagan-Thatcher deregulation of financial markets removes the capital controls that had limited cross-border investment since Bretton Woods. The World Trade Organization (successor to the GATT, founded 1995 CE) expands the reach of trade liberalization to services, intellectual property, and agricultural products. Container shipping — standardized cargo containers that can move seamlessly between ships, trains, and trucks — reduces transoceanic freight costs by roughly 90% from the 1950s to the 1990s CE, making globally distributed supply chains economically viable. Digital communications make coordinating those supply chains operationally feasible.

---

### Three key developments

**1. The great divergence reversed: emerging market growth**

Global Integration's most important consequence is the most underreported major news story of the late 20th and early 21st centuries: the largest reduction in absolute poverty in human history. The World Bank estimates that the share of the global population living on less than $1.90 per day falls from roughly 36% in 1990 CE to roughly 10% by 2015 CE — approximately a billion people lifted above the extreme poverty line. China accounts for the majority of this reduction, but India, Indonesia, Bangladesh, Vietnam, and much of sub-Saharan Africa also show dramatic improvements.

This is globalization's genuine achievement — and it is real, measurable, and historically extraordinary. The economic convergence between the global South and North, stalled for most of the 20th century, resumes in the globalization era at a pace that has no historical precedent in peacetime. The working class of the developing world — factory workers in Shenzhen, garment workers in Dhaka, software engineers in Bangalore — are globalization's primary beneficiaries in terms of absolute income gains.

**2. The hollowing of the Western middle class**

Globalization's gains are not evenly distributed within rich countries, and the distributional consequences are politically explosive. The "elephant graph" — economists Branko Milanovic's visualization of income growth across the global distribution between 1988 and 2008 CE — shows income gains concentrated at the global bottom (emerging market workers) and the global top (wealthy elites in rich countries), with a conspicuous hollow in the middle: the working and lower-middle class of the industrial democracies, whose real incomes stagnate while their consumption is sustained by cheap imports and debt.

The mechanism is the "China shock" and its equivalents: manufacturing jobs in tradeable sectors move to lower-wage locations, leaving behind communities whose entire social and economic infrastructure was organized around industries that no longer exist there. The communities don't recover — the empirical evidence is clear that displaced manufacturing workers in their 40s and 50s typically don't find equivalent employment, and the social pathologies that follow — opioid addiction, declining life expectancy among non-college white Americans, the collapse of community institutions — are the human face of globalization's distributional failure.

**3. Supply chains, interdependence, and fragility**

Global Integration produces supply chains of extraordinary efficiency and extraordinary fragility. The "just-in-time" manufacturing model — pioneered by Toyota and adopted globally — eliminates inventory buffers in favor of continuous delivery, reducing costs but removing any slack in the system. Semiconductors fabricated in Taiwan, assembled in South Korea, installed in devices manufactured in China, sold globally through American platforms — the modern supply chain is a marvel of coordinated efficiency with no redundancy.

COVID-19 demonstrates what happens when such a system encounters a shock it wasn't designed for: global supply chains for medical equipment, semiconductors, and basic consumer goods break simultaneously in 2020–2021 CE, producing shortages in wealthy countries unaccustomed to them. The subsequent rethinking — "reshoring," "friend-shoring," strategic stockpiling — represents the first systematic effort to rebuild redundancy into global supply chains since the 1970s. The efficiency-resilience tradeoff, ignored during the decades of globalization's momentum, is now a central question of industrial policy.

---

### Why it matters

Global Integration matters because it defines the economic and political conditions of the contemporary world. Its achievements — lifting a billion people from poverty, connecting the world economy, producing the consumer abundance of the late 20th century — are real and should not be minimized. Its failures — the hollowing of industrial communities in rich countries, the concentration of gains at the very top of the income distribution, the fragility of over-optimized supply chains, and the political backlash that all of this generates — are equally real and equally consequential. The political movements that dominate the 2010s and 2020s — Brexit, Trumpism, the global rise of economic nationalism — are, at their core, the political expression of globalization's distributional failures. Understanding Global Integration is understanding where the 21st century's central political conflicts come from.
