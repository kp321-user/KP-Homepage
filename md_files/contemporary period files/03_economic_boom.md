# Economic Boom
### Post-War Era · Contemporary (~1950–1973 CE)

The post-war Economic Boom — the *Trente Glorieuses* (thirty glorious years) as the French call it, or simply the Golden Age of capitalism — is the most sustained period of broadly shared economic growth in the history of industrial societies. Between 1950 and 1973 CE, Western European economies grow at an average of roughly 5% annually, Japanese GDP grows at nearly 10% annually, and American living standards double. Unemployment is effectively zero across the industrial democracies for most of this period. The working class of the richest countries achieves, for the first time in history, genuine material comfort — cars, refrigerators, televisions, holidays, homeownership — previously available only to the wealthy. It is an extraordinary and historically anomalous period whose end is as instructive as its duration.

---

### The structural foundations

The boom's foundations are multiple and mutually reinforcing. The Bretton Woods monetary system — fixed exchange rates pegged to a dollar convertible to gold — provides international monetary stability that supports trade growth. American hegemony provides security guarantees that allow Western Europe and Japan to spend minimally on defense. The Marshall Plan's institutional legacy — European economic cooperation, trade liberalization — creates the framework for the European Common Market (1957 CE), the world's largest single market. Cheap oil — the price of oil in real terms is lower in 1970 CE than in 1950 CE — powers industrial growth. And the post-war baby boom provides expanding consumer markets and growing labor forces simultaneously.

The Keynesian policy framework — governments managing aggregate demand through fiscal and monetary policy to maintain full employment — operates effectively in this period, not because Keynesian theory is always correct but because the structural conditions favor it: growing populations, technological catch-up from the pre-war period, the spread of mass production techniques, and the demand generated by rebuilding war-shattered infrastructure.

---

### Three key developments

**1. The consumer society reaches the working class**

The boom's most socially transformative consequence is the democratization of consumption. In 1950 CE, the automobile, the refrigerator, and the television are middle-class luxuries in Europe and aspirational goals for American workers. By 1970 CE, they are standard household items across the working class of every industrial democracy. The physical landscape transforms accordingly: suburban sprawl expands around every major city as car ownership makes commuting from cheaper land possible; supermarkets replace local markets as refrigerators make weekly shopping viable; television creates genuinely mass shared culture for the first time.

The implications for gender are significant and contradictory. Labor-saving domestic appliances reduce the labor of housework substantially — but in a context where women are still largely confined to domestic roles, they also raise the standards expected of housewives. The "feminine mystique" that Betty Friedan diagnoses in 1963 CE — the suffocation of educated women in a consumer domesticity that offers comfort but not meaning — is a specifically boom-era condition, enabled by the prosperity that makes one-income households viable.

**2. The European project**

The Economic Boom is the material foundation of European integration. The European Coal and Steel Community (1951 CE), the Treaty of Rome (1957 CE) creating the European Economic Community, and the progressive expansion of the Common Market through the 1960s are simultaneously economic projects (expanding the market available to European producers) and political projects (making European war economically unthinkable by integrating the economies of France and Germany so thoroughly that conflict becomes mutually destructive).

The EEC's economic logic works: trade between member states grows faster than trade with the outside world, and the gains from integration are broadly distributed. The political logic also works, in the specific sense intended: France and Germany do not go to war again, and the prospect becomes genuinely inconceivable to subsequent generations in a way it was not to those who lived through 1914 and 1939. The European project is the boom era's most durable institutional achievement.

**3. The end of the boom: 1973 and its consequences**

The boom ends with a precision that makes its causes identifiable. The 1973 Arab-Israeli War triggers the OPEC oil embargo, quadrupling oil prices in months. The Bretton Woods system has already collapsed in 1971 CE when Nixon suspends dollar-gold convertibility, ending the monetary framework that underpinned the boom's international stability. Simultaneously, the productivity gains from post-war technological catch-up are exhausted; inflation, suppressed during the boom years, begins accelerating across the industrial democracies.

The result is "stagflation" — simultaneous high inflation and high unemployment, which Keynesian theory says should be impossible and for which Keynesian policy has no remedy. The intellectual and political response — monetarism, supply-side economics, the Reagan-Thatcher revolution of the 1980s CE — dismantles the post-war settlement's key institutions: weakening trade unions, liberalizing financial markets, reducing social spending, and accepting higher unemployment as the price of lower inflation. The boom's end is not just an economic event; it is the trigger for a political transformation whose effects are still reverberating.

---

### Why it matters

The post-war Economic Boom matters because it demonstrates that broadly shared prosperity is achievable — that capitalism's tendency toward inequality is not inevitable but can be moderated by institutional design. It also matters because its end generates the political backlash that has defined Western politics since the 1980s: the progressive dismantling of the institutions that made the boom possible, the rise of inequality, the erosion of the middle class, and the political resentments that fuel populism. The boom is the benchmark against which subsequent economic performance is measured and found wanting — particularly by the working-class voters in former industrial regions whose living standards peaked in the early 1970s and have not recovered their relative position since.
