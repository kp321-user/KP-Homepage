# Decolonization
### Post-War Era · Contemporary (~1945–1975 CE)

Decolonization is the 20th century's largest political transformation — the dismantling of the European overseas empires that had controlled roughly 85% of the Earth's land surface and the emergence of approximately 80 new independent states in thirty years. It is simultaneously a story of liberation and disappointment, of principle and betrayal, of genuine self-determination and the exchange of formal colonialism for informal economic dependency. The world it creates — the political map of Africa, Asia, and the Middle East — is the world we still inhabit, with all the structural inheritances that entails.

---

### Why it happens when it does

Decolonization's timing is not accidental. The Second World War fatally weakens the European imperial powers — Britain and France emerge victorious but economically exhausted and militarily dependent on the United States, which has its own ideological and strategic reasons to oppose formal colonialism (the Declaration of Independence is awkward to defend while governing India). The war also mobilizes millions of colonial soldiers who return home with military training, political consciousness, and the lived experience of having fought for freedoms their own societies are denied.

The Atlantic Charter (1941 CE) — signed by Roosevelt and Churchill — commits both powers to "the right of all peoples to choose the form of government under which they will live." Churchill insists this applies only to European peoples under Nazi occupation; colonial subjects hear it as a universal commitment. The UN Charter (1945 CE) reaffirms self-determination as a universal right. The moral case for empire, never philosophically coherent, collapses under the weight of principles the imperial powers themselves have proclaimed.

---

### Three key developments

**1. South Asia: partition and its consequences**

India's independence (August 15, 1947 CE) is the decolonization wave's most significant early event — the jewel of the British Empire achieving independence peacefully through Gandhi's mass movement and Nehru's constitutional politics. The partition that accompanies independence — dividing the subcontinent into Hindu-majority India and Muslim-majority Pakistan along hastily drawn borders — produces the 20th century's largest forced migration: approximately 14 million people displaced and between 200,000 and 2 million killed in communal violence in the Punjab and Bengal.

The partition's logic — that religious identity is the appropriate basis for national borders — produces consequences that are still being lived with: three India-Pakistan wars, a nuclear standoff, the Kashmir dispute, and the creation of Bangladesh (1971 CE) through East Pakistan's bloody secession. Partition is the British Empire's most damaging departing act — the borders drawn in six weeks by a British lawyer (Cyril Radcliffe) who had never visited India, using inadequate maps, produce conflicts that have lasted seventy-five years and counting.

**2. Africa: the flag independence and its limits**

African decolonization accelerates dramatically after 1957 CE, when Ghana under Kwame Nkrumah becomes the first sub-Saharan African country to achieve independence. By 1966 CE, the vast majority of African colonies are formally independent. Harold Macmillan's "Wind of Change" speech to the South African Parliament in February 1960 CE — acknowledging that African national consciousness is a political fact that must be accommodated — signals British recognition that empire is no longer sustainable.

The manner of decolonization matters enormously. In most of British Africa and French West Africa, independence is negotiated relatively peacefully through constitutional processes. In Algeria (1954–1962 CE), Kenya (Mau Mau uprising, 1952–1960 CE), and Portuguese Africa (independence not achieved until 1974–1975 CE), independence comes through armed struggle against colonial powers unwilling to negotiate. The French-Algerian War kills an estimated 300,000–400,000 people, destabilizes the French Fourth Republic, and brings de Gaulle back to power — its domestic French consequences are as consequential as its Algerian ones.

The structural problem is that formal political independence does not automatically produce economic independence. The colonial economies — organized to export raw materials and import manufactured goods, with local elites educated in colonial languages and trained in colonial administrative traditions — are not easily redirected toward self-sustaining development. The term "neo-colonialism" — coined by Nkrumah — describes the persistence of economic dependency after political independence: the same extractive relationships, now mediated through international corporations and financial institutions rather than colonial administrations.

**3. Indochina and the violence of decolonization**

Vietnam is decolonization's most devastating case. The Vietnamese independence movement under Ho Chi Minh, having fought Japanese occupation during the war, declares independence in September 1945 CE — citing the American Declaration of Independence in its founding document. France's refusal to accept Vietnamese independence produces the First Indochina War (1946–1954 CE), ending in French defeat at Dien Bien Phu and the Geneva Accords temporarily dividing Vietnam at the 17th parallel.

American misreading of Vietnamese nationalism as primarily communist expansion — rather than primarily anti-colonial independence — produces the American intervention that becomes the Vietnam War (1955–1975 CE): 58,000 American deaths, approximately 2–3 million Vietnamese deaths, and the most consequential American foreign policy failure of the Cold War era. The fall of Saigon on April 30, 1975 CE, ends the war and completes Vietnamese unification — the outcome Ho Chi Minh sought in 1945 that three decades of war and millions of deaths could not prevent.

---

### Why it matters

Decolonization matters because it creates the contemporary world's political geography and most of its unresolved conflicts. The borders drawn by colonial powers — in the Middle East, Africa, and South Asia — are the structural foundations of conflicts that show no sign of resolution. The economic relationships established during the colonial period — primary commodity export, manufactured goods import, debt to Northern financial institutions — persist in modified forms as the structural basis of global inequality. Decolonization is not completed history; it is the ongoing context within which the majority of the world's population lives and its politics are conducted.
