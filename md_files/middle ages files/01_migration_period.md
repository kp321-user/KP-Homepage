# Migration Period
### Early Middle Ages · Middle Ages (~376–568 CE)

The Migration Period is history written in movement. Within roughly two centuries, the ethnic and political map of Europe is redrawn almost beyond recognition — Roman provinces become Germanic kingdoms, old identities dissolve, new ones form, and the civilization that emerges from the wreckage is neither Roman nor barbarian but something genuinely new. It is one of the most consequential demographic reshufflings in Western history.

---

### The trigger: the Huns

The cascade begins on the Eurasian steppe. Around 370 CE, the Huns — a nomadic confederation from Central Asia, formidable horse archers whose origins remain debated — sweep westward and collide with the Gothic peoples living north of the Black Sea. The Visigoths, facing destruction, petition the Roman Emperor Valens in 376 CE to cross the Danube and settle within the empire as refugees. Valens agrees. Roman officials then abuse and exploit the desperate Goths so severely that they revolt — and at the Battle of Adrianople in 378 CE, a Gothic army kills Valens and destroys a Roman field army. It is the first major Roman military defeat in generations, and it signals that the empire can no longer control its own frontiers.

What follows is not a single invasion but a cascading series of migrations, each pushing the next: Huns push Goths, Goths push other Germanic peoples, everyone pushes against Roman borders simultaneously.

---

### Three key developments

**1. The Germanic kingdoms**

As Roman central authority in the West collapses through the 5th century, Germanic groups fill the vacuum — not by destroying Roman civilization but by inheriting and partially preserving it. The Visigoths settle in Spain and southern Gaul. The Vandals cross into North Africa, seizing the grain supply that had fed Rome for centuries. The Angles and Saxons move into Britain as Roman legions withdraw. The Franks consolidate control of Gaul. The Ostrogoths, under Theodoric the Great, rule Italy from Ravenna with conspicuous respect for Roman law and administration.

None of these groups want to destroy Rome — they want to *be* Rome, or at least benefit from its prestige and institutions. Theodoric corresponds in Latin, employs Roman administrators (including the philosopher Boethius, whom he later executes on suspicion of treason), and maintains Roman legal structures alongside Gothic custom. The result is a hybrid civilization that is the direct ancestor of medieval Europe.

**2. Attila and the Hunnic Empire**

Attila (~434–453 CE) briefly makes the Huns the most feared power in the known world. From a base on the Hungarian plain, he extracts enormous tribute from Constantinople, devastates the Balkans, invades Gaul (turned back at the Battle of the Catalaunian Plains, 451 CE, in one of antiquity's largest battles), and threatens Italy before withdrawing. His empire collapses almost immediately after his death in 453 CE — held together by personal authority rather than institutional structure, it fragments among his sons within a generation. The Hunnic moment is a demonstration that nomadic confederations can achieve spectacular short-term impact but rarely produce durable states.

**3. The survival of the Eastern Empire**

While the Western empire dissolves, Constantinople survives and will continue to survive for another thousand years. The Eastern Roman (Byzantine) Empire has several structural advantages: wealthier provinces, shorter and more defensible frontiers, a continuous administrative tradition, and the Bosphorus as a natural choke point that no migration-era army could cross without a navy. The East's survival is not inevitable — it faces the same external pressures — but its greater institutional resilience makes the difference. Byzantium will be the custodian of Greek and Roman learning through the darkest centuries of the West's contraction.

---

### What is actually lost

The Migration Period's costs are real. Urban populations across the Western empire collapse dramatically — Rome itself shrinks from perhaps a million inhabitants to under 50,000 by the 6th century. Long-distance trade contracts. Literacy rates fall. The sophisticated Roman agricultural and hydraulic infrastructure degrades as the administrative capacity to maintain it disappears. The 5th and 6th centuries in western Europe are genuinely darker than what preceded them, not in the caricatured sense of total ignorance, but in the measurable sense of reduced complexity, connectivity, and material standard of living for most people.

---

### Why it matters

The Migration Period is the founding trauma and founding act of medieval Europe simultaneously. The political geography it creates — France from the Franks, England from the Angles, Spain from the Visigoths — is recognizably the ancestor of the modern map. The cultural synthesis it begins — Germanic social structures fused with Roman law and Christian institutions — is the matrix from which medieval civilization grows. It is not a story of destruction followed by rebuilding from nothing. It is a story of transformation, painful and uneven, that preserves more than it destroys and creates something genuinely new in the process.
